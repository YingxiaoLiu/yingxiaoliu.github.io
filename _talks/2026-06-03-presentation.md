Presentation at the Engineering Mechanics Institute Conference (EMI 2026)

<p style="text-align: justify;">
Freezing and thawing of soils involve coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical processes, pronounced rate dependence, and evolving interactions among soil grains, pore water, and ice. These mechanisms govern the deformation and strength evolution of soils and thus determine their long-term stability in both natural cold-region environments and engineered freezing applications such as artificial ground freezing (AGF). Despite significant progress, reliably predicting time-dependent freeze heave and thaw settlement remains challenging because existing models often capture phase change but do not fully represent the evolving loadtransfer mechanisms among the constituent phases.
</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">
We present a unified computational framework based on a generalized effective stress theory that explicitly accounts for the transition of the ice phase from fluid-like crystals suspended in pore water to solid-like inclusions forming part of the load-bearing skeleton. This transition is modeled through a dual-skeleton formulation that enables a smooth shift from an unsaturated-flow regime to a fully frozen composite regime. By incorporating the rate-dependent mechanical behavior and creep of ice, the framework captures both short-term freezing dynamics and long-term deformation during sustained freezing. 
</p>
<p style="text-align: justify;">
The proposed model is implemented within a fully coupled finite element formulation and validated against laboratory-scale freeze-thaw experiments and a field-scale AGF tunneling project involving multi-stage freezing and excavation. Numerical simulations accurately reproduce the evolution of freezing fronts, surface heave, and time-dependent deformation observed in practice, and are further used to evaluate alternative freezing strategies.
