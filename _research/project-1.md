<p style="text-align: justify;">
Our research group develops coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical models to investigate the behavior of freezing and frozen soils under changing environmental and mechanical conditions. Freezing soils involve strong interactions among heat transfer, water migration, ice formation, pore pressure evolution, and deformation of the soil skeleton. These coupled processes play an important role in cold-region infrastructure, artificial ground freezing, frost heave, thaw settlement, and permafrost degradation under climate change.
</p>

<p style="text-align: justify;">
A key component of this work is the development of a modified effective stress theory for frozen soils. In partially frozen soils, the mechanical role of ice evolves with temperature, ice content, and microstructure. When ice crystals are small, they are suspended in the pore water and move together with the liquid phase; in this regime, ice primarily contributes to the pore-fluid response. As freezing progresses and ice crystals grow, they begin to contact soil particles and form load-bearing ice bridges. At this stage, ice becomes part of the solid skeleton and directly contributes to the stiffness and strength of the frozen soil. Our modified effective stress framework accounts for this transition by describing the changing contribution of ice from a pore-fluid component to a load-bearing solid component.
</p>

<p style="text-align: justify;">
We formulate and implement physics-based numerical models that describe the transition between unfrozen, partially frozen, and frozen states. The models account for phase change, unfrozen water content, cryogenic suction, ice-water interactions, and the evolving mechanical response of the soil as temperature changes. By integrating thermal, hydraulic, and mechanical processes within a unified finite element framework, we aim to capture both short-term freezing and thawing behavior and long-term deformation of frozen ground.
</p>

<p style="text-align: justify;">
Our numerical framework has been validated against artificial ground freezing model tests, where the evolution of temperature fields, freezing fronts, water redistribution, and soil deformation can be measured under controlled laboratory conditions. These comparisons allow us to assess the model’s ability to reproduce key coupled processes during freezing and thawing and to improve its predictive capability for engineering applications involving artificial ground freezing and cold-region construction.
</p>

<p style="text-align: justify;">
This work provides a computational platform for studying how temperature variation, water redistribution, ice content, loading, and soil properties influence the performance of frozen soils. The resulting models can be used to analyze laboratory experiments, interpret field observations, and support the design of resilient infrastructure in cold regions.
</p>
